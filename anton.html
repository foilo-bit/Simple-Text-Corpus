Слово о пълку Игоревѣ, Игоря сына Святъславля, внука Ольгова.

Не лѣпо ли ны бяшетъ, братіе, начяти старыми словесы трудныхъ повѣстій о пълку Игоревѣ, Игоря Святъславлича! Начати же ся тъй пѣсни по былинамь сего времени, а не по замышленію Бояню. Боянъ бо вѣщій, аще кому хотяше пѣснь творити, то растѣкашется мыслію по древу, сѣрымъ вълкомъ по земли, шизымъ орломъ подъ облакы.

Поход

Почнемъ же, братіе, повѣсть сію отъ стараго Владимера до нынѣшняго Игоря; иже истягну умь крѣпостію своею, и поостри сердца своего мужествомъ, наплънився ратнаго духа, наведе своя храбрыя пълкы на землю Половѣцькую за землю Руськую.

Тогда Игорь възрѣ на свѣтлое солнце и видѣ отъ него тьмою вся своя воя прикрыты, и рече Игорь къ дружинѣ своей: "Братіе и дружино! Луцежъ бы потяту быти, неже полонену быти. А всядемъ, братіе, на свои бръзыя комони, да позримъ синего Дону."

Битва

Съ заранія въ пятъкъ потопташа поганыя пълкы Половецкыя; и рассушясь стрѣлами по полю, помчаша красныя дѣвкы Половецкыя, а съ ними злато, и паволокы, и драгыя оксамиты.

Другаго дни велми рано кровавыя зори свѣтъ повѣдаютъ; чръныя тучя съ моря идутъ, хотятъ прикрыти д̃ солнца. Се вѣтри, Стрибожи внуци, вѣютъ съ моря стрѣлами на храбрыя пълкы Игоревы!

"Яръ туре Всеволодѣ! стоиши на борони, прыщеши на вои стрѣлами, гремлеши о шеломы мечи харалужными."

Поражение

Бишася день, бишася другый: третьяго дни къ полуднію падоша стязи Игоревы. Ту ся брата разлучиста на брезѣ быстрой Каялы. Ту кроваваго вина недоста; ту пиръ докончаша храбріи Русичи: сваты попоиша, а сами полегоша за землю Рускую.

Ничить трава жалощами, а древо стугою къ земли преклонилось. Уже бо, братіе, невеселая година въстала, уже пустыни силу прикрыла.

Злато слово Святъслава

Тогда Великій Святславъ изрони злато слово слезами смѣшено, и рече: "О моя сыновчя Игорю и Всеволоде! рано еста начата Половецкую землю мечи цвѣлити, а себѣ славы искати. Но нечестно одолѣста: нечестно бо кровь поганую проліяста."

Плачь Ярославны

Ярославна рано плачетъ въ Путивлѣ на забралѣ, аркучи: "О вѣтрѣ! вѣтрило! чему, Господине, насильно вѣеши? Чему мычеши Хиновьскыя стрѣлкы на своею нетрудною крилцю на моея лады вои?"

Ярославна рано плачеть Путивлю городу на заборолѣ, аркучи: "О Днепре словутицю! ты пробилъ еси каменныя горы сквозѣ землю Половецкую. Ты лелѣялъ еси на себѣ Святославли носады до пълку Кобякова: възлелѣй, Господине, мою ладу къ мнѣ, а быхъ не слала къ нему слезъ на море рано."

Ярославна рано плачетъ къ Путивлѣ на забралѣ, аркучи: "Свѣтлое и тресвѣтлое слънце! всѣмъ тепло и красно еси: чему, Господине, простеръ горячую свою лучю на ладѣ вои?"

Побѣгъ Игоря

Прысну море полунощи; идутъ сморци мьглами; Игореви Князю Богъ путь кажетъ изъ земли Половецкой на землю Рускую, къ отню злату столу.

Донецъ рече: "Княже Игорю! не мало ти величія, а Кончаку нелюбія, а Руской земли веселіа."

Игорь рече: "О, Донче! не мало ти величія, лелѣявшу Князя на вълнахъ, стлавшу ему зелѣну траву на своихъ сребреныхъ брезѣхъ, одѣвавшу его теплыми мъглами подъ сѣнію зелену древу."

Возвращение

Солнце свѣтится на небесѣ, Игорь Князь въ Руской земли. Дѣвици поютъ на Дунаи. Вьются голоси чрезъ море до Кіева. Игорь ѣдетъ по Боричеву къ Святѣй Богородици Пирогощей.

Страны ради, грады веселі, пѣвше пѣснь старымъ Княземъ, а по томъ молодымъ. Пѣти слава Игорю Святъславлича. Буй туру Всеволодѣ, Владимиіру Игоревичу. Здрави Князи и дружина, побарая за христьяны на поганыя пълкы. Княземъ слава, а дружинѣ Аминь.

English Translation (1918)

HEARKEN, my brethren, while I tell you the tale of Igor, son of Svyatoslav. In the ancient way shall it be told, not after the fashion of the immortal Boyan but in manner seemly, following the legends of the time.

For when Boyan made a song for men to hear, his thoughts leaped swiftly like a squirrel in the tree-tops, or ran like a grey wolf over the plain or like an eagle, soared beneath the clouds.

The Campaign

Let me tell then, brethren, the tale of Igor. Behold his soul was filled with warlike spirit and mighty was his mind. His heart he whetted keen upon the stone of courage and naught would content him but that he should lead his valiant host to the land of the Polovtsi, to do honour to Russia his country.

Then Igor cast his eyes upon the sun and saw that its splendour was dimmed, so that his men stood all veiled in shadow. But the soul of Igor was aflame and his thirst for adventure so great that he heeded not the evil omen.

Igor cried to his warriors:

"Brothers and fellow warriors! It were better to die than to be oppressed. Mount, my warriors! Let us speed onward to the blue stream of the Don."

The Battle

At break of day the battle commenced and by God's will the Russians prevailed. The foul swarms of the Polovtsi were crushed into the earth. Their sons were slain and their fair daughters captured and a great booty was taken from them.

On the morrow the dawn arose in bloodstained raiment to proclaim the day. Dark clouds rolled heavily up from the sea, laden with blue, quivering flashes of lightning. The rain fell like arrows from the sky and the winds, children of Stribog, blew as keen daggers.

Vsevolod, fierce as a wild boar, stood foremost in the fray. Wherever shone the gold of his helmet there was the fiercest fighting and Polovetsky heads covered the ground.

The Defeat

The call of the trumpets, bidding the armies to retire, was heard at dawn, but only at noon on the third day was the banner of Igor lowered. The prince was taken prisoner. From a king's saddle he fell to that of a slave, and thus on the shores of the Kayala were the brothers parted.

The grass wept for grief and the trees were bowed in mourning.

Svyatoslav's Golden Word

Svyatoslav the Golden-tongued, wept, saying:

"Alas! my children! Too early did ye wish for fame and too early journeyed ye forth, seeking to conquer the land of the Polovtsi! No honour could come of it. Your brave hearts were bound in iron and forged in the fire of valour, but ye have brought sorrow upon my silvered head."

Yaroslavna's Lament

Yaroslavna, the beloved of Igor, arose. She went upon the walls of Putivl and wept.

"Wind, Sir Wind! Why art thou so keen? Why dost thou blow so cruelly upon my beloved? Cease the beating of thy great wings, Sir Wind!"

And Yaroslavna looked upon the river.

"O Mighty Dnieper! Thou didst divide the stone mountains in the country of the Polovtsi! Bring my beloved back to me, Sir River! Ah! Woeful was the day when I first wept for him!"

And Yaroslavna looked upon the sun at dawn.

"Bright Sun! Thou art so warm and beautiful. To all men art thou kind. And yet, Sir Sun, thou didst punish my beloved with cruel flames."

Igor's Escape

The sea rose up like a fountain of blue cloud and Igor knew it for a sign from heaven that, by God's will, the time had come for his escape.

At early dawn Igor reached the river Donets. And the river spake to him, saying:

"Great, indeed, art thou, O Prince, and great is the hope of Russia, but great also is the hatred of Kontchak!"

Igor replied:

"But mighty art thou, Donets! Thy waves carry the prince right nobly and by thy silver shores has he found a fair couch of green."

The Triumphant Return

What bliss was there in Russia, brethren, when Igor reached the land of his fathers and knelt before the Holy Virgin at Pirogosk! The sun shone more brightly. The maidens sang: "Joy! Joy!" and the sound of their rejoicing was heard from the Danube to Kiev.

Honour to Prince Igor, Honour to brave Vsevolod, And honour to their children. Honour to the princes And honour to their warriors! Amen.

Орьтъмами и япончицами, и кожухы начашя мосты мостити по болотомъ и грязивымъ мѣстомъ, и всякыми узорочьи Половѣцкыми. Бишася день, бишася другый: третьяго дни къ полуднію падоша стязи Игоревы. Ту ся брата разлучиста на брезѣ быстрой Каялы. Ту кроваваго вина недоста; ту пиръ докончаша храбріи Русичи: сваты попоиша, а сами полегоша за землю Рускую.

Ярославна рано плачетъ къ Путивлѣ на забралѣ, аркучи: "Свѣтлое и тресвѣтлое слънце! всѣмъ тепло и красно еси: чему, Господине, простеръ горячую свою лучю на ладѣ вои? въ полѣ безводнѣ жаждею имь лучи съпряже, тугою имъ тули затче."

English Translation (1918)

HEARKEN, my brethren, while I tell you the tale of Igor, son of Svyatoslav. In the ancient way shall it be told, not after the fashion of the immortal Boyan but in manner seemly, following the legends of the time.

For when Boyan made a song for men to hear, his thoughts leaped swiftly like a squirrel in the tree-tops, or ran like a grey wolf over the plain or like an eagle, soared beneath the clouds. He sent forth falcons to pursue a flock of swans and the first swan that was captured sang its song, a song to Yaroslav the Old, or to Mstislav the Brave who put Rededia to the sword before the tents of Kassog; a song again to Roman, the beauteous son of Svyatoslav. But Boyan, brethren, did not in truth send forth falcons to pursue a flock of swans but with magic fingers he would touch the chords which of themselves would chant the princes' praise in sonorous music.

The Campaign

Let me tell then, brethren, the tale of Igor. Behold his soul was filled with warlike spirit and mighty was his mind. His heart he whetted keen upon the stone of courage and naught would content him but that he should lead his valiant host to the land of the Polovtsi, to do honour to Russia his country.

Then Igor cast his eyes upon the sun and saw that its splendour was dimmed, so that his men stood all veiled in shadow. But the soul of Igor was aflame and his thirst for adventure so great that he heeded not the evil omen.

Igor cried to his warriors:

"Brothers and fellow warriors! It were better to die than to be oppressed. Mount, my warriors! Let us speed onward to the blue stream of the Don. Have I not vowed to break my lance upon the uttermost ends of the Polovetsky plains? Though my head be brought even to the ground, yet would I drink in my helmet of the waters of the Don!"

The Battle

At break of day the battle commenced and by God's will the Russians prevailed. The foul swarms of the Polovtsi were crushed into the earth. Their sons were slain and their fair daughters captured and a great booty was taken from them, of rich stuffs and gold and fine linen.

On the morrow the dawn arose in bloodstained raiment to proclaim the day. Dark clouds rolled heavily up from the sea, laden with blue, quivering flashes of lightning. The thunder clouds quickly hid the four suns and a terrible storm broke. The rain fell like arrows from the sky and the winds, children of Stribog, blew as keen daggers.

Vsevolod, fierce as a wild boar, stood foremost in the fray. Wherever shone the gold of his helmet there was the fiercest fighting and Polovetsky heads covered the ground. His arrows showered upon the foe, his good sword crashed upon their helms.

The Defeat

The call of the trumpets, bidding the armies to retire, was heard at dawn, but only at noon on the third day was the banner of Igor lowered. The prince was taken prisoner. From a king's saddle he fell to that of a slave, and thus on the shores of the Kayala were the brothers parted. Great had been the feast and deep the drinking of the wine of blood! The Russians had in truth glutted their guests with wine. Nevertheless, many were dead in defence of Russia. The grass wept for grief and the trees were bowed in mourning.

Svyatoslav's Golden Word

Svyatoslav the Golden-tongued, wept, saying:

"Alas! my children! Too early did ye wish for fame and too early journeyed ye forth, seeking to conquer the land of the Polovtsi! No honour could come of it. Your brave hearts were bound in iron and forged in the fire of valour, but ye have brought sorrow upon my silvered head. The good wrought by my brother, the mighty, the rich Yaroslav, is no more."

Yaroslavna's Lament

Yaroslavna, the beloved of Igor, arose. She went upon the walls of Putivl and wept.

"Wind, Sir Wind! Why art thou so keen? Why dost thou blow so cruelly upon my beloved? Cease the beating of thy great wings, Sir Wind! To send sorrow from the clouds is but a little thing to thee, who tosseth the boats on the blue sea so easily! Take not my joy from me! Blow it not away across the plain!"

And Yaroslavna looked upon the river.

"O Mighty Dnieper! Thou didst divide the stone mountains in the country of the Polovtsi! Thou didst carry the ships of Svyatoslav to the armies of Kobyak. Bring my beloved back to me, Sir River! Ah! Woeful was the day when I first wept for him!"

And Yaroslavna looked upon the sun at dawn.

"Bright Sun! Thou art so warm and beautiful. To all men art thou kind. And yet, Sir Sun, thou didst punish my beloved with cruel flames. In the waterless fields thou didst parch his bowstrings so that they could not bend for thirst, and thou didst fill his quivers with sorrow. Ah! Woeful was the day when first I wept for him."

Igor's Escape

Now shall we speak again of Igor, brethren, as he lay captive in the Polovetsky tents by the river. All day long he dreamed of freedom. His spirit slept not but was ever on the wing, measuring the distance between the Don and the Donets, which is the frontier stream of Russia.

And behold after many days, the sea rose up like a fountain of blue cloud and Igor knew it for a sign from heaven that, by God's will, the time had come for his escape.

At early dawn Igor reached the river Donets. And the river spake to him, saying:

"Great, indeed, art thou, O Prince, and great is the hope of Russia, but great also is the hatred of Kontchak!"

Igor replied:

"But mighty art thou, Donets! Thy waves carry the prince right nobly and by thy silver shores has he found a fair couch of green. With soft mists hast thou hidden him beneath the green trees. The Triumphant Return

What bliss was there in Russia, brethren, when Igor reached the land of his fathers and knelt before the Holy Virgin at Pirogosk! Henceforth did the warriors prosper and win great battles against the pagans. The sun shone more brightly. The maidens sang: "Joy! Joy!" and the sound of their rejoicing was heard from the Danube to Kiev. The cities, too, and all the land, took up the joyful song. For all time shall it be sung:

Honour to Prince Igor, Honour to brave Vsevolod, And honour to their children. Honour to the princes And honour to their warriors! Amen.
